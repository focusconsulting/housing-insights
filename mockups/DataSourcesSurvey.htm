Type of Data Use Potential Sources
Cost to preserve Can provide an indicator of the magnitude of investment required, for comparison to actual spending allocations. - City tax records
- Comparison of tax record to market prices
Distance to DC funded economic development - Specific criteria of an 'economic development project' TBD, from city records
Distance to transit Indicates both value to residents, as well as an early indicator of potential risk (being close to metro will often indicate being more likely to increase neighborhood market rents) - Metro locations and high-traffic bus stops
Inspection scores - Already available in the Preservation Catalog (REAC scores)
Market rents and home prices By comparing this to the rent received by the building owner we can have an indicator of the risk of loss in a neighborhood. - Zillow price data by neighborhood
- American Community Survey data
Neighborhood Demographics - Census data
Owner details (property records) Useful when specific owners are known to the preservation community to behave in certain ways.  - Register of deeds
- If possible, tracing LLC registrations to find actual owner lists
Quantity nearby affordable units Indicate whether preserving a building is needed to maintain economic diversity in a neighborhood. - Preservation Catalog properties
- Additional buildings not yet covered in the Catalog
Resident Type The typical income level of residents as well as information like elderly and disabled residents. - Subsidy type
- Other data TBD
TOPA notices We can incorporate items like email notifications when TOPA notifications are issued, so that everyone in the preservation community can be aware of current developments.  - TBD if this is possible to obtain systematically
Upcoming private neighborhood investment Rent and home price data are neighborhood level; this provides a more specific indicator of potential pressure to sell a property due to changes within a few block radius..  -Planned Unit Development Process records
- Nearby building permits
